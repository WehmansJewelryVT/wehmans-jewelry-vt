The 4 C’s of Diamonds and Other Gemstones and why They are Important

A Guide to Quality and Value

At Wehman’s Jewelry LLC, we believe an informed customer is a happy one. Whether you are looking for a diamond or a precious gemstone, understanding these four factors will help you find the perfect balance of beauty and value.

1.) Cut

The cut isn't about the shape, but how well a diamond or gemstone's facets interact with light. A well-cut gemstone reflects light internally from one facet to another and disperses it through the top. Although when it does come down to the cut for precious gemstones, cabochons are typically worth half as much as say a gemstone that was brilliant round or facet cut.

Why it matters: Cut is the first of the four C's because it determines the sparkle and "fire" of the stone, but doesn't always justify price.

2. Color

For white diamonds, the "Color" grade actually refers to the lack of color. The GIA scale ranges from D (colorless) to Z (light yellow or brown).

Pro Tip: Many "near-colorless" stones (G-J) look colorless to the naked eye but offer much better value, allowing you to invest more in the Cut.

For other precious gemstones, they are graded on a universal color scale also known as the "Color Intensity Scale". This scale ranges from light to dark. A light color intensity means that the gemstone appears very light in color and almost pale. A medium-light color intensity means the gemstone appears kind of pale but overall still has a little bit of saturation. A medium color intensity means the gemstone appears kind of pale but also kind of saturated. An intense color intensity means that the gemstone appears saturated but also a little pale. A vivid color intensity is the most desirable color intensity as it's known for very rare varities like pigeon blood ruby, royal blue sapphire, and vivid green emerald, amongst others. A vivid color intensity is defined as being very saturated but not too saturated to where it looks almost black. Finally, a dark color intensity means a gemstone that appears very saturated or dark in color almost appearing blackish blue common blue sapphires or dark blackish red for rubies. This is what the color factor means and it's much more important in determining value than cut is.

But, that's not all.

3. Clarity

Clarity measures the number, size, and placement of internal characteristics called "inclusions" and external marks called "blemishes."

Our Philosophy: We focus on "eye-clean" stones—pieces that may have small inclusions under a microscope but look flawless to you and your loved ones.

4. Carat Weight

Carat refers to the physical weight of the diamond, not its size. Two diamonds of equal carat weight can have very different values depending on the other three C's.

The Wehman Way: We help you find the "sweet spot" where the stone looks impressive and fits your budget perfectly.
